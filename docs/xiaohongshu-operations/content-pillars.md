# Xiaohongshu Content Pillars for aDict

## 1. Local Dictionary Ownership

Audience: users who already have dictionary files or care about local-first lookup.

Angles:

- Why local dictionaries still matter when AI lookup exists.
- How aDict handles user-provided dictionary files.
- What MDict and StarDict support means in real daily use.
- Privacy and control without turning the post into abstract security marketing.

## 2. Reading Workflow

Audience: people who read English, Japanese, technical articles, papers, docs, or books on Apple devices.

Angles:

- Look up a word without breaking reading flow.
- Save useful words with context.
- Compare dictionary result, suggestion, and original text.
- Use aDict as a quiet companion instead of a study dashboard.

## 3. AI-Assisted Lookup

Audience: users curious about Apple Intelligence or AI explanations but still needing dependable dictionary data.

Angles:

- AI can explain, dictionary data anchors the answer.
- When to use explanation versus exact dictionary entries.
- How aDict 3.0 frames AI as enrichment, not replacement.
- Real examples where a dictionary entry prevents vague AI wording.

## 4. Product Rebuild Story

Audience: indie-app followers, Apple users, and productivity-tool users.

Angles:

- Why an old dictionary app deserved a full rewrite.
- What changed from the older aDict versions.
- Design tradeoffs behind menus, sources, and sessions.
- The difference between adding features and rebuilding the lookup architecture.

## 5. Practical Setup

Audience: potential users who need concrete confidence before trying the app.

Angles:

- First setup after installing TestFlight or App Store build.
- Importing or organizing dictionary files.
- Common MDict or StarDict file layout questions.
- What is supported now and what is planned later.
